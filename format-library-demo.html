1. Closed-form questions

Simple right-or-wrong questions. Kid clicks a button or types a short answer.

1.1Single-correct MCQ

4 options, only one is right. Kid taps one. Wrong shakes; right turns green.

Demo
When are ice cream sales highest?
Right — both lines peak in summer.

1.2Multi-correct MCQ

Several options, multiple are right. Kid checks all that apply, then taps Check.

Demo
Which of these are facts the ice cream / shark chart actually shows? (Pick all that are true.)
Right. The chart only shows what happened — "cause" is a story the headline added.

1.3True / False

A statement and two buttons. Kid taps one.

Demo
"Greenland is bigger than Africa." — True or false?
False. Africa is 14× the size of Greenland — the Mercator projection makes Greenland look huge.

1.4Fill-in-the-blank

Sentence with a missing word. Kid types — or taps a word chip — to slot it into the blank.

Demo · type the answer
On a Mercator map, the lie is biggest at the and bottom.
Right. Mercator stretches the top and bottom of the map most.
Demo · tap a word chip
When two things move together, ask if a is driving both.
Right. There's almost always a hidden third thing behind two things rising together.

1.5Numeric input

Kid types a number. Accepts an exact answer or a small range.

Demo
Africa is _____ × bigger than Greenland.
×
Accepts 12–16. Truth: 14×.
Right — Africa is ~14× the size of Greenland.

2. Manipulation questions

Still right-or-wrong, but the input is physical: drag, tap, or sort objects on screen.

2.1Match the following

Two columns. Kid pairs items by tapping one on the left, then one on the right (or by dragging).

Demo · tap left, then tap right
Match each map projection to its best use.
All matched. Each map is good at one job and bad at others — that's model thinking.

2.2Sort / arrange in order

Items shuffled at top. Kid drags or taps up/down arrows to put them in order — by size, time, or sequence.

Demo
Sort biggest → smallest by real area.
Right. Africa > Australia > India > Greenland — the Mercator map made them look completely different.

2.3Drag to category (bucket sort)

Items at top, 2–3 buckets below. Kid drags each item into the right bucket.

2.4Tap to identify

An image with several regions. Kid taps the right region.

Demo · tap the misleading part of the chart
Tap the part of this chart that's the trick.
Right. The chart shows only 3 months — a cherry-picked window. Roblox actually has yearly cycles.

3. Soft framing

No right answer. Anchor the kid's stance before the investigation begins.

3.1Gut check

3 buttons, no wrong answer. Each pick gets a tailored mascot reply.

Demo
Reading just a 3-month "Roblox dying" chart — does it convince you?

3.2Sense check

1–2 quick MCQs that confirm the kid can read the chart before they're asked to judge it.

Demo · two chained reads
Looking at the orange line — when are ice cream sales highest?
Now the red line — when are shark attacks highest?
Both peak in summer. You can read the chart — now what do you make of it?

4. Investigation patterns

Multi-step exploration with mascot guidance. The mascot reacts but doesn't give answers away.

4.1Tap-to-explore + commit

Phase 1: tap each clue and hear the mascot's take. Phase 2: pick one as the real trick.

Demo · explore each clue
Pick this Roblox chart apart. Tap each piece — Lens has a take on each.
—
Of all four — which is the real trick?
Right. The 3-month window is what makes a normal seasonal dip look like a death spiral.

4.2Hypothesis tester

Kid tests multiple guesses. Wrong ones grey out with reasons. Only the right one unlocks the reveal.

Demo · test each hypothesis
What's driving BOTH ice cream and shark attacks up together?
—
Found it. Summer drives both — neither causes the other. That's the Third Thing trick.

4.3Fact vs claim sort

Statements at top, 2 buckets (FACT / CLAIM). Kid drags each into the right bucket.

Demo · drag each statement
Which of these is what the data shows, and which is what the headline added?
Both lines rise in summer
Ice cream causes shark attacks
Shark attacks dropped to 0 in winter
Sharks love sweet smells
Fact (data shows)
Claim (headline added)
Right. The chart only shows what — not why. Causation is always a story added on top.

5. Immersive interactions

The centerpieces. Kid manipulates an object or system; the math runs in real time under the gesture.

5.1Drag-to-transform

Kid drags an object on a canvas. As it moves, it visibly transforms using real math.

Demo · drag the country down toward the equator
Drag "Greenland" to the equator. Watch it shrink to its real size.

GREENLAND

The Projection Lens

Every flat picture of a complex world lies about something. When you see a model — a map, a chart, a summary — ask: what got preserved? what got sacrificed? who decided?
