Skill: Verifying claims about size. Someone says "this is half" — can you check if that's true?
Core question: "I split it in half!" — Did you really?
Why this matters
"Half" is the first claim kids hear every day. They need to know how to check it.
AI can measure for you. But the instinct to question "is that really half?" — that's yours. This activity builds it through food portions kids immediately care about.

How does a K-1 kid check "half"?
🔲
Overlay — put one piece on top of the other. Do they match?
🫗
Pour — pour into identical cups. Same level = same amount.
No grids. No counting squares. No math. Just: do the two pieces match?

Screen 2 Round 1 — Cookie
"I broke the cookie in half!"
Two pieces — one clearly bigger. Kid commits. Overlay check → NOT half. Easy start, gut is right.
  1. Cookie appears whole, then snaps into two uneven pieces with a crunch sound
  2. Claim text appears: "I broke it in half!"
  3. Verdict buttons appear — kid taps "Yes, it's half" or "No way!"
  4. After kid commits → "Let's check!" button appears. No take-backs.
  5. Kid taps check → small piece slides on top of big piece with a gentle animation
  6. Gap between pieces highlights in red. Arrow + "gap!" label fades in
  7. Result text: "Small piece doesn't cover the big one. NOT half!"
  8. Rule 1 card animates in from bottom and stays at screen bottom
  9. If kid got it right → confetti burst + "Nice catch!" · If wrong → "Hmm, let's look again" + replay overlay animation

Interaction Design — Key Patterns
Gut Before Math
Kid commits to "half" or "not half" BEFORE the check tool runs. The gap between gut and reality is the lesson.
No Take-Backs
Once kid taps a verdict, it locks. This creates stakes. Changing your mind after seeing the answer doesn't teach anything.
Rule Stacking
Each round adds a rule card to the growing stack. By Screen 6, kid has 4 tools. Transfer round tests if they can pick the right one.
Physical Check Only
K-1 kids can't do area math. So every check is physical: overlay (solids) or pour (liquids). No numbers, no grids.
Audio & Feedback
🔊 Overlay match Soft "click" when pieces align perfectly (like puzzle piece snapping) 🔊 Overlay gap Low "buzz" when pieces don't match, gap highlights red 🔊 Pour Water pouring sound while liquid fills the measuring cups 🔊 Rule earned "Ding!" when new rule card flips in — sense of progress 🔊 Surprise Playful "whoa!" sound when kid's gut was wrong — not punitive 🔊 Verdict lock Subtle stamp/click sound when kid taps their answer — confirms it's locked
Transitions Between Screens
Screen → Screen: Current food item shrinks to its icon and slides into a "done" tray at top. Next food icon enlarges into the new scene. Keeps context that this is one canteen visit, not separate activities.
Verdict → Check: 0.3s pause after kid commits. Then "Let's check!" button appears. Kid taps to trigger the overlay/pour animation.
Check → Rule: Result text fades in (0.5s), then rule card slides up from bottom (0.3s). "Next →" button appears after rule card settles.
Round → Toolkit (S6): All 4 food icons fly into a row. Rules flip in below with staggered delay.

Open / To Refine

  • Overlay interaction: Does kid drag the piece to overlay, or does it animate on tap? Drag feels more physical but harder on small screens. Recommendation: tap to trigger, with option to drag for explorers.
  • Pour animation: Simple tilt-and-fill (faster to build, clear result) vs. realistic liquid physics (more delightful but complex). Recommend simple.
  • Difficulty ramp: Cookie (S2) might be too easy — could start at sandwich to jump straight into surprise. But cookie builds confidence and teaches the overlay tool, so probably keep it.
  • Transfer round count: 2 claims currently. Could add a 3rd (e.g., cake cut into 3 uneven pieces, "everyone got a piece so it's equal!") to test Rule 4 independently.
  • Replay value: Same scenarios every time. Could randomize claim details (different food, same mechanic) for repeat plays.
  • Context banner: Persistent "School Canteen" header at top of all screens? Keeps the story alive but takes vertical space.
  • Voiceover: For K-1 kids, should claims be read aloud? Would reduce reading dependency but adds production cost.
